Mouthful of Dust – a line taken from Ned Kelly’s Jerilderie Letter – presents spatial photography of his armour, boot, rifle, death mask and the Letter, accompanied by new writing commissions from Grace Chan, Cameron Hurst of The Paris End, Wesley Enoch, Sarah Krasnostein, and Nam Le. Made from hundreds of still photographs of these 19th-century objects, the 3D models offer near-forensic detail and uncanny perspectives. Across a spectrum of literary genres, the writers describe what they observe in these images now floating in digital space, and what they imagine as they navigate around them. Their stories and speculations add new layers of interpretation and suggest different ways of knowing these historic objects.

Edward ‘Ned’ Kelly, Australia’s most notorious bushranger, was born in 1854, the same year State Library Victoria was founded. His short life was a series of crises, some self-made but many imposed and his story remains intertwined with the Library through our collections. Ultimately hanged for murder, Kelly orchestrated daring campaigns of theft and destruction while claiming he was unjustly outlawed. His actions, as well as those of his pursuers, and the whole setting for his story, have been discussed for over a century.

For many, including some of Australia’s best-known artists and writers, Kelly is seen as a person of independent thought, who experienced but also forcefully denounced racial and class persecution, and abuses of power. Novelist Peter Carey described Kelly as an avant-garde artist after reading his famous Jerilderie Letter, and he has inspired iconic phrases and images in our culture: the shape of an iron helmet with a horizontal slit belongs to no-one else. Carey’s award-winning novel True History of the Kelly Gang  argues, along with its epigraph by William Faulkner, that ‘The past is not dead. It is not even past.’ Kelly’s ongoing relevance as a figure of resistance is enlivened by this idea.

Grace Chan

Grace Chan is an award-winning speculative fiction writer. She writes about brains, minds, and space. Her critically acclaimed debut novel, Every Version of You, is about staying in love after mind-uploading into virtual reality. It won the NSW Premier’s Literary Awards People’s Choice Award and was shortlisted for The Age Book of the Year and longlisted for the Stella Prize and the Indie Book Awards. Her short fiction can be found in Clarkesworld, Lightspeed, Going Down Swinging and many other places. Grace was born in Malaysia and lives in Melbourne. In her other life, she works as a psychiatrist.

Cameron Hurst - The Paris End

Cameron Hurst is a writer and founding editor of The Paris End. The Paris End is Melbourne's finest weekly newsletter of arts, culture, and goings-on around town, and has been covered by The New York Times, The Age, and The Conversation, among other publications. Hurst is also a PhD candidate in the Art History program at the University of Melbourne and a contributing editor of Memo Review and Index Press.

theparisend.substack.com

Wesley Enoch

Wesley Enoch is the former Artistic Director at the Sydney Festival. Wesley has written and directed iconic Indigenous productions The 7 Stages of Grieving, Black Medea and The Story of The Miracles at Cookie’s Table. He has directed productions of The Sapphires, Black Diggers, I Am Eora, The Man from Mukinupin, Yibiyung, A Raisin in The Sun, and The Visitors. Wesley is the current Professor of Indigenous Practice, Creative Industries QUT, the Co-Chair of Annamila First Nations Foundation, the Deputy Chair of Creative Australia and the Patrick White Fellow for the Sydney Theatre Company.

Sarah Krasnostein

Sarah Krasnostein is a multi-award winning writer and critic. She is the best-selling author of The Trauma Cleaner, The Believer, the Quarterly Essay, Not Waving, Drowning and On Peter Carey. Her awards include the Victorian Prize for Literature, the Australian Book Industry Award for Non-Fiction and the Walkley’s Pascall Prize for Arts Criticism. She is a regular contributor to The Monthly and The Saturday Paper. She holds a doctorate in criminal law and is admitted to legal practice in Australia and America.

sarahkrasnostein.com

Nam Le

Nam Le’s work encompasses fiction, non-fiction, poetry and works on screen. His debut poetry collection 36 Ways of Writing a Vietnamese Poem was published in March 2024 in Australia, the US and UK Poems from it appeared in Poetry, Paris Review, Granta, American Poetry Review, BOMB, HEAT, Overland and elsewhere. Le’s work has been translated into over 14 languages and has received major awards internationally, including the PEN/Malamud Award, the Dylan Thomas Prize, the Australian Prime Minister’s Literary Award and the Melbourne Prize for Literature. Le's work has been published in Penguin’s Modern Classics series and is widely anthologised and taught. He lives in Melbourne.
